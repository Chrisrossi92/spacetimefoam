What is This Theory?

Imagine the fabric of space isn't smooth—it's bubbly like foam at tiny scales due to quantum effects. This "spacetime foam" could subtly distort light from the distant universe, making the expansion rate (Hubble constant) look different when measured far away vs. nearby. This explains the 8-10% mismatch scientists see without needing new physics.

Backed by quantum gravity ideas from John Wheeler and data from telescopes like Hubble and Planck. It's testable with upcoming missions like JWST.

  • Early Universe (CMB): Light travels farther, more foam distortion → Lower H0 (~67 km/s/Mpc).
  • Nearby (Supernovae): Less distortion → Higher H0 (~73 km/s/Mpc).

References: Wheeler (1955) on foam; Planck (2018) and SH0ES (2024) data.
